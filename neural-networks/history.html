The Birth of Neural Networks

From the mind–body problem to backpropagation — a history in seven moves.

Mental phenomena can be described by interconnected networks of simple and often uniform units. This article traces how that idea emerged — from Cajal's neurons and Russell's logic, through the Perceptron and the AI Winter, to backpropagation and deep learning — treating each breakthrough as the answer to a question left open by the one before it.

The Problem That Started Everything

Before there were neural networks, there was a scandal: the mind existed, and nobody could explain it.

For centuries, the dominant view was dualism — mind and body are made of different stuff. Descartes put the soul in the pineal gland and moved on. Nobody had a better answer.

Then three separate fields converged on the same problem from different directions:

  • The Biologist's piece: Cajal established the brain was discrete individual cells — neurons — each separated by a tiny gap (the synapse). But how does a series of electrical sparks become a thought?
  • The Logician's piece: Whitehead and Russell proved all mathematics could be built from three logical primitives: AND, OR, NOT.
  • The Collision: Walter Pitts saw what nobody had connected: if logic can represent mathematics, and neurons can represent logic — then neurons can represent everything. The mind wasn't separate from the body — it was the body's wiring diagram.

This was the generative insight. Everything else is a consequence.

1943 — McCulloch & Pitts: The Brain as Logic Machine

Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts formalized the collision in "A Logical Calculus of Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity." Their core claim: a neuron "realizes" a logical sentence if its firing pattern (On/Off) matches the True/False values of that sentence.

The Idealization

Real neurons are messy: graded potentials, variable thresholds, continuous time, chemical soup. McCulloch and Pitts made a deliberate sacrifice: strip the neuron to a binary abstraction. Fire or don't fire. One or zero.

Why was this the right trade? Binary firing = Boolean algebra. Boolean algebra can express any finite logical statement. What you lose: biological realism. What you gain: mathematical power.Boole published Laws of Thought in 1854. Russell and Whitehead built on it directly in Principia Mathematica (1910–13).

Since AND + OR + NOT can express any logical statement (Principia Mathematica), a network of neurons can compute anything a Turing machine can compute. The geometry of connections is the software.

What M-P left unexplored: the connections were hand-wired. There was no learning. Can the connections be learned from experience?

1949 — Hebb: The First Learning Rule

Donald Hebb proposed a simple principle:

"Neurons that fire together, wire together."

Formally: \Delta w_{ij} \propto x_i \cdot x_j

The crack in the foundation: Hebb's rule has no error signal. It adjusts based on what fires together, not whether the network was right. It can build associations but cannot correct itself.

1958 — Rosenblatt: The Perceptron Learns

Rosenblatt introduced one revolutionary addition: variable weights that update based on mistakes.

The Geometry of a Weight

Think of each input as a dimension. Two inputs → each example is a point in 2D space. The weights define a line — a knife cutting the plane in two. Learning = rotating that knife until it correctly separates all the data.

The bias term b lets the boundary slide freely anywhere in the plane. The full computation: w \cdot x + b \geq 0.

The update rule:

w_{i}^{\text{new}} = w_{i}^{\text{old}} + \alpha\,(y - \hat{y})\,x_i \qquad b^{\text{new}} = b^{\text{old}} + \alpha\,(y - \hat{y})

When the perceptron is right, (y-\hat{y})=0 and nothing changes. When wrong, the weights shift in proportion to the input that caused the mistake.

The Convergence Theorem

Rosenblatt proved: if a separating solution exists, the algorithm will find it in finite steps.The proof uses the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality. Two quantities compete: alignment with the ideal weights grows linearly with mistakes, while the norm grows as √mistakes — so the cosine of the angle between them must eventually reach 1.

What Rosenblatt left unexplored: the linearity constraint. The knife can only be a straight line. What if no straight line separates the data?

1969 — Minsky & Papert: The Crisis

Minsky and Papert proved: a single-layer perceptron can only solve linearly separable problems.

The crucial distinction: M-P proved you can build XOR with a hand-wired multi-layer circuit. Minsky & Papert proved a single-layer network cannot learn the weights for XOR. Multi-layer networks could represent anything — but nobody knew how to train them. This is the credit assignment problem: how do you propagate error backward through hidden layers?

The Hidden Layer Insight

The solution was geometrically obvious once seen: if one layer can only draw straight lines, multiple layers can warp the space itself before drawing the final line.

The Three Breakthroughs of the 1980s

A. Backpropagation

Training is finding the lowest valley in a loss landscape. Gradient descent measures the local slope and steps downhill. The challenge: how do you compute the gradient when weights are buried inside many composed functions?

The chain rule decomposes the gradient layer by layer:

\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial w}

Each term is a local derivative — easy to compute at each layer. The backward pass multiplies them together. Error flows backward, assigning proportional blame to every weight. This wasn't a trick: given the compositional structure of a neural network, the chain rule is the only tool that decomposes the gradient correctly.

Sigmoid's derivative peaks at 0.25. Chain-multiply through 10 layers:

Universal Approximation

A neural network with one hidden layer and enough neurons can approximate any continuous function to arbitrary precision.Proved independently by Cybenko (1989) for sigmoid activations and Hornik (1991) for general non-polynomial activations.

The representational question is settled. But notice what this does not guarantee: how big the network needs to be; that you can learn the right weights; or that it will generalize. These three gaps — efficiency, learnability, generalization — are what the entire subsequent history of ML is working to close.

The Why-Regress

Why do neural networks work? → Universal Approximation: they can represent any function.

Why can they represent any function? → Non-linearity lets them warp space; depth lets them compose transformations.

Why does warping space help? → Any classification is a geometry question. Transform the space until the classes are linearly separable, then a single cut solves it.

Why is linear separability the right primitive? → Because it's what a single threshold unit can compute. The whole architecture is built to make the final cut easy.

Why does data have structure a network can exploit? → The world has regularities. Without regularity, no learning algorithm works. Neural networks are machines for extracting and encoding that structure.
