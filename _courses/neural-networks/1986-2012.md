## The Ideas in Conflict

The AI Winter didn't freeze everyone equally. By the early 1980s, **expert systems** were the dominant paradigm — commercially deployed, DARPA-funded, institutionally respectable. The approach: hire a domain expert, interview them exhaustively, encode their knowledge as explicit IF-THEN rules, run an inference engine over them. XCON, deployed at Digital Equipment Corporation in 1982, was saving the company $40M a year.[^expertsystems]

The competing ideas were:

- **Connectionism** — the underground view that intelligence emerges from learning, not from hand-crafted rules. The PDP (Parallel Distributed Processing) group at UCSD — Rumelhart,[^rumelhart] McClelland, Hinton[^hinton_toronto] — were its intellectual centre. Their 1986 two-volume manifesto *Parallel Distributed Processing* was a declaration of war.
- **Statistics** — a separate discipline entirely. Statisticians had logistic regression, linear discriminant analysis, and maximum likelihood. They considered neural networks unprincipled black boxes with no theoretical guarantees.
- **Control theory** — Widrow and Hoff's ADALINE (1960) had used gradient descent on single-layer networks. The mathematics was there; nobody had extended it to multiple layers.

The question that separated these camps: **does intelligence come from knowledge representation, or from learning?** Expert systems said: encode what you know. Connectionists said: learn it from data.

The expert systems camp had funding, prestige, and commercial success. The connectionists had a better idea.

## 1986 — Backpropagation: The Chain Rule as Credit Assignment

The problem Minsky left open in 1969 was **credit assignment**: given an error at the output, how do you apportion blame to hidden units deep in the network? A hidden unit's error isn't directly observable — you only see the final output. How does the signal get back?

### The Ideas Rosenblatt Was Working Against (Redux)

The block wasn't mathematical difficulty. The chain rule is 200 years old. The block was conceptual:

- Nobody believed multi-layer networks were worth training. Minsky's verdict had poisoned the well.
- Credit assignment seemed to require **global coordination** — some central controller surveying the whole network, deciding who was responsible. Local units computing their own blame seemed circular.

The insight that broke the impasse: **the architecture is a composition of functions.** Each layer applies a function to the previous layer's output. The derivative of a composition is — by the chain rule — a product of local derivatives. Each unit only needs to know its own local gradient and the gradient flowing in from the layer above.

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial w}$$

No global coordination required. The forward pass computes activations layer by layer; the backward pass computes gradients layer by layer, in reverse. The error *flows backward* through the same structure the activations flowed forward through.

This wasn't a trick. It was the only tool that correctly decomposes the gradient given the compositional structure. The architecture already contained the solution. The same structure that made credit assignment seem impossible made it uniquely tractable.[^backprop][^werbos]

## Non-Linear Activations: Why Linear Layers Are Useless Stacked

If you stack linear layers: $W_2(W_1 x) = (W_2 W_1)x$ — still one big matrix. No matter how many layers, you are still cutting space with a single straight line. Depth without non-linearity collapses.

Non-linearity introduces **kinks** that let the network learn curved, complex boundaries. Each activation breaks the linearity at that layer, so subsequent layers compose *genuinely different* transformations.

The competing ideas about which non-linearity to use:

- **Sigmoid** (1986) — biologically motivated, smooth, bounded. Problem: derivative peaks at 0.25. This mattered enormously once networks went deep.
- **Tanh** (zero-centred sigmoid) — a small improvement. Same peak derivative problem.
- **ReLU** — *Rectified Linear Unit*, $\max(0, x)$. Known in neuroscience for decades (biological neurons are roughly ReLU-shaped). Nobody thought to use it in artificial networks until Glorot & Bengio (2010) analysed why sigmoid was failing, and Nair & Hinton (2010) showed ReLU worked better in practice.[^relu]

## The Vanishing Gradient: Why Depth Still Failed

Backprop solved multi-layer *training*. It did not solve deep networks. The field discovered this the hard way through the late 1980s and 1990s.

### The Problem

Sigmoid's derivative peaks at **0.25**. Chain-multiply through 10 layers:

$$0.25^{10} \approx 10^{-6}$$

Early layers receive nearly zero gradient. They don't learn. The network is theoretically deep but effectively shallow — only the last few layers are actually training. The further back you go, the less information arrives.[^hochreiter]

### The Fixes

The fixes arrived over two decades from three separate communities:

| Era | Problem | Fix | Why it works |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | Shrinking gradients | **ReLU** | Derivative = 1 for positive inputs — no compounding decay |
| 2015 | Internal covariate shift | **Batch Normalisation** | Re-centres and rescales activations at each layer; gradient flow stabilised |
| 2015 | Deep gradient death | **Residual connections**[^residual] | Skip-connections route gradient directly to earlier layers, bypassing the chain entirely |

None of these arrived together. ReLU required recognising sigmoid as the culprit. BatchNorm came from a Google team noticing training became unstable as depth increased. ResNets arose from an anomaly: adding more layers was *making performance worse*, not better — which shouldn't happen if the extra layers could simply learn identity mappings.[^residual]

## Universal Approximation

**A neural network with one hidden layer and enough neurons, using a non-linear activation, can approximate any continuous function to arbitrary precision.**<span class="sidenote">Proved independently by Cybenko (1989) for sigmoid activations and Hornik (1991) for general activations.</span>

The proof is constructive: each neuron carves out a "bump" in the input space. Stack enough bumps, you can approximate any shape.

But notice what Universal Approximation does *not* guarantee:

- How *large* the network needs to be (could be exponentially large)
- That you can *learn* the right weights (existence ≠ findability)
- That the network will *generalise* to new data

These three gaps — efficiency, learnability, generalisation — define the open problems of the next 30 years.

## 1989–1998 — LeCun and the CNN Thread

The vanishing gradient problem stalled deep *fully-connected* networks. But Yann LeCun found a different path: **exploit the structure of the input domain**.

### The Ideas in Conflict

The competing approaches to vision in the late 1980s:

- **Hand-crafted features** — edge detectors, HOG descriptors, SIFT. Computer vision researchers spent years designing features by hand based on their understanding of image structure. Learning was not part of the pipeline; features were engineered, then fed to a classifier.
- **Fully-connected networks** — treat each pixel independently. An FC network must learn that a cat's ear looks the same whether it appears top-left or centre-right — redundantly, for every position.
- **LeCun's insight**: natural images have *spatial structure*. Patterns that matter (edges, textures, shapes) appear at multiple locations. The same detector should work everywhere — **share weights across positions**.

### The Insight

A **convolution** is a filter that scans across the input, applying the same small set of weights at every position. One filter learns one kind of pattern (an edge, a curve, a texture) and finds it *wherever it appears*.

This buys two things:

1. **Translation equivariance** — if the pattern moves, the activation moves with it. The network doesn't have to re-learn "cat ear in top-left" separately from "cat ear in centre."
2. **Parameter efficiency** — instead of a weight connecting every input pixel to every hidden unit ($784 \times 500 = 392{,}000$ parameters for a tiny MNIST network), a $5 \times 5$ filter has 25 parameters and applies them everywhere.

Stack convolution layers and you get a hierarchy: the first layer learns edges, the second learns textures built from edges, the third learns shapes built from textures. Each layer abstracts over the layer before.[^lecun]

### What LeNet Actually Did

LeCun's LeNet (1989, refined to LeNet-5 in 1998) was not a research curiosity. It was deployed in real systems: by the late 1990s, LeNet was reading handwritten cheques at ATMs across the United States. Deep learning was running production systems while the academic field was still debating whether neural networks worked at all.[^lenet]

The architecture:

```
Input (32×32) → Conv → Pool → Conv → Pool → FC → FC → Output (10 classes)
```

Each conv-pool block reduces spatial size while increasing depth. The FC layers at the end combine learned features to produce a classification.

### What It Left Open

CNNs worked because images have spatial structure. The same principle — **exploit domain structure through architectural inductive bias** — would take decades to apply elsewhere:

- Text has *sequential* structure → RNNs, then Transformers
- Graphs have *relational* structure → GNNs
- Physics has *symmetry* structure → equivariant networks

The CNN insight is really a meta-insight: **the right inductive bias, baked into the architecture, can substitute for enormous amounts of data and compute**. This remained the dominant design principle until scale made it optional.

## What This Era Left Open

The backprop era solved the training problem for shallow networks and, with CNNs, for structured domains. What remained:

- **Scale**: LeNet worked on small images (32×32). Real-world images are orders of magnitude larger. Training on them required compute that didn't exist yet.
- **Data**: Supervised learning requires labels. Getting millions of labelled examples requires infrastructure that also didn't exist yet.
- **Depth for unstructured domains**: Fully-connected deep networks still failed on raw pixels at scale. The vanishing gradient fixes (ReLU, BatchNorm, ResNets) arrived in 2010–2015 — not yet.
- **Generalisation theory**: Why do these networks generalise to unseen data? Especially: why do *overparameterised* networks generalise? This question remains genuinely open.

The door was briefly reopened in 2006 by Hinton's deep belief network paper,[^hinton2006] which showed that greedy layer-wise pretraining could give gradient descent a starting point good enough to train deep networks. Pretraining turned out to be unnecessary once better activations and initialisation arrived — but it bought six years of renewed research. The compute and data problems were solved together in 2012 — the story of AlexNet, ImageNet, and the GPU revolution.[^dnnresearch] That is the next era.

[^expertsystems]: Expert systems peaked commercially in the early 1980s. The "Fifth Generation" project, a Japanese government initiative to build AI hardware, prompted DARPA to fund a competing US programme — the Strategic Computing Initiative. Companies like Symbolics, IntelliCorp, and Teknowledge raised significant venture capital. By 1987 the market had collapsed: the hardware (Lisp machines) was being undercut by general-purpose workstations, and the fundamental limitations of encoding knowledge manually had become apparent. The expert system winter hit just as backpropagation was published. The two failures — of knowledge engineering and of shallow connectionism — cleared the ground for something new.

[^backprop]: Backpropagation was not invented by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams — it was rediscovered by them. Paul Werbos derived it in his [1974 Harvard PhD thesis](https://gwern.net/doc/ai/nn/1974-werbos.pdf) and got essentially no response. David Parker rediscovered it in 1982. Yann LeCun independently derived it in 1985. Rumelhart's 1986 *[Nature](https://gwern.net/doc/ai/nn/1986-rumelhart-2.pdf)* paper received the credit largely because of timing and Hinton's institutional prominence. Werbos spent years watching others get credit for his work. The history of ideas is full of this: the idea that gets remembered is not always the first one, but the one that lands in fertile ground.

[^werbos]: What led Werbos to backpropagation was not control theory but Freud. In a [1993 interview](https://gwern.net/doc/ai/nn/rnn/1998-werbos.pdf), Werbos described his motivating idea as "translating Freud's theory of psychic energy into mathematics" — Freud's idea of a backward flow of credit through a psychological system. By 1971–72, at Harvard, this had become explicit derivative equations. His Harvard committee rejected the work in stages. When he presented it to Larry Ho (of the control theorists Bryson and Ho): *"Yeah, yeah, but you know, we're not convinced it's so straightforward. You got to prove some theorems first."* When he produced a simplified version, the committee said: *"Look, this will work, but this is too trivial and simple to be worthy of a Harvard PhD thesis."* When he proposed the full model, the committee said: *"This is just unacceptable. This is crazy, this is megalomaniac, this is nutzoid."* He went to Minsky, who refused to sponsor him. He went to Grossberg, who said: *"It's already been done before, or else it's wrong — I'm not sure which of the two, but I know it's one of the two."* His department chairman told him he could not stay unless he abandoned the area. He survived by embedding backprop secretly inside a political science thesis on forecasting social conflict, eating soybean soup and chicken neck soup in a Roxbury slum apartment to conserve money. He got his degree. He then could not publish the algorithm clearly for eight more years. When Rumelhart's 1986 paper appeared — the same derivation — it received 20,000 citations. The historical irony is acute: the Larry Ho who had dismissed Werbos's thesis is now sometimes credited with the same algorithm. *"There are now some people who are saying backprop was invented by Bryson and Ho. They don't realize it was the same Larry Ho, who was on my committee and who said this wasn't going to work."*

[^rumelhart]: David Rumelhart — first author of the 1986 backprop paper and intellectual leader of the PDP movement — developed Pick's disease (frontotemporal dementia) in the mid-1990s, a few years after arriving at Stanford. By 1998 he could no longer work; he retired as professor emeritus and moved to Michigan to live with his brother. He died in 2011 at 68, unable to speak or write for the last decade of his life. The Rumelhart Prize — the field's highest honour, named after him — was established in 2001. Its first recipient was Geoffrey Hinton. Rumelhart was alive but already incapacitated; he could not receive the prize himself or know what the field he had revived would become. He died one year before AlexNet.

[^hinton_toronto]: Hinton's move to the University of Toronto in 1987 was deliberate. He had been at Carnegie Mellon, then collaborated at UCSD. He left for Canada partly on principle: DARPA was the dominant funder of AI research in the United States, and Hinton refused to take money with defence applications. Toronto offered a position with no such strings. He stayed for 25 years. The students who passed through his lab — Yann LeCun, Brendan Frey, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Ilya Sutskever, Alex Krizhevsky — built the modern field. The principled retreat that looked like obscurity was the founding of an intellectual dynasty.

[^relu]: The story of ReLU is a lesson in how long it takes an obvious idea to become obvious. Relu-like activations ($\max(0, x)$) were known in neuroscience modelling since the 1960s — neurons don't fire below threshold and fire proportionally above it. Hinton used a noisy version (noisily rectified linear unit) in 2010. Glorot and Bengio's 2010 paper "Understanding the difficulty of training deep feedforward neural networks" showed *why* sigmoid was failing (symmetry-breaking and gradient saturation). Nair and Hinton (2010) showed empirically that ReLU outperformed sigmoid. Krizhevsky used ReLU in AlexNet (2012) and put the result on the map. An activation function that matches the biology was largely ignored by the machine learning community for 50 years.

[^residual]: He et al.'s observation was this: take a 20-layer network and a 56-layer network. The deeper one performs *worse* on training data. This should be impossible if the extra layers could just learn the identity function ($f(x) = x$) and pass the signal through unchanged. The fact that they couldn't suggested gradient flow was breaking down before the weight update even began. The residual connection — $F(x) + x$ instead of $F(x)$ — reframes the problem: instead of learning the full transformation, learn the *residual* (the difference from identity). Learning to output near-zero is easier than learning to reproduce the input exactly. The skip connection also gives gradients a direct highway to early layers, bypassing the chain multiplication entirely.

[^lecun]: LeCun arrived at Bell Labs in 1988 after a postdoc under Hinton in Toronto, where he had begun experimenting with weight-sharing. His account of the insight is characteristically undramatic: it was "the logical thing to do." If the same feature matters at multiple positions in an image, why learn it separately for each position? One filter, scanning everywhere. He traced the architectural idea to Hubel and Wiesel's 1962 discovery of oriented edge detectors in cat visual cortex — the [1989 paper](https://direct.mit.edu/neco/article/1/4/541/5515) explicitly cites them. But Hubel and Wiesel's cells had to be hand-wired; LeCun's could be *learned* end-to-end with backprop. That was the combination nobody had made. LeCun spent the 1990s largely ignored by the broader ML community, which had turned to SVMs following Cortes and Vapnik (1995). SVMs had elegant convex optimisation and proofs; neural networks had engineering instinct and empirical results. LeCun kept publishing. He moved to NYU in 2003, still against the current. The 2012 ImageNet result vindicated him definitively.

[^lenet]: The extent of early neural network deployment is often forgotten. AT&T Bell Labs estimates that by the late 1990s, LeNet or its descendants were processing 10–20% of all cheques written in the United States. Deep learning was not merely a laboratory curiosity — it was running critical financial infrastructure. This fact was largely invisible to the broader ML research community, which had moved on to SVMs and ensemble methods. The practical success of LeNet in production did not translate into academic interest, because academic interest tracks theory and benchmarks, not deployed systems.

[^hochreiter]: Hochreiter's [1991 diploma thesis](https://www.bioinf.jku.at/publications/older/2304.pdf) at TU Munich contained a rigorous mathematical proof of the vanishing gradient problem. The core result: in a recurrent network, error signals scale as a product of terms of the form *f'(net) × w* at each time step. With sigmoid activations, *f'* peaks at 0.25. If weights are small enough to prevent explosion, the product is less than 1 at every step — meaning error decays exponentially with sequence length. Increasing the weights makes things worse, not better: as weights grow, *f'* shrinks toward zero, keeping the product near zero. The problem is not incidental; it is structural. The thesis was written in German, by an unknown student, and received almost no attention. The solution Hochreiter and Schmidhuber published six years later — the [LSTM (1997)](https://direct.mit.edu/neco/article/9/8/1735/6109/Long-Short-Term-Memory) — was built around what they called the **constant error carousel**: a memory cell with an identity self-connection (weight exactly 1.0), so the gradient neither grows nor shrinks as it circulates. Gates — multiplicative units — control what enters and leaves the cell. The 1997 paper received roughly 10–20 citations per year through 2000. It now has ~98,000 citations, making it one of the most cited papers in machine learning history. The delay was partly practical: compute in 1997 was approximately ten million times more expensive than today, making the experiments that would demonstrate LSTM's advantages prohibitively slow to run.

[^hinton2006]: By 2006, deep networks were still considered untrainable beyond two or three layers — the vanishing gradient had not yet been beaten by ReLU or BatchNorm. Hinton's [2006 paper](https://www.cs.toronto.edu/~fritz/absps/ncfast.pdf) with Osindero and Teh found a different approach: instead of training all layers jointly from random initialisation (which failed), train each layer independently as a *generative model* first, then fine-tune the whole stack with backprop. A Restricted Boltzmann Machine (RBM) trained layer by layer gave gradient descent a good starting point — good enough that the signal didn't vanish before reaching the early layers. The key mathematical insight was *complementary priors*: if you design the prior over one layer to match the posterior the layer below computes, the "explaining away" problem that had made deep inference intractable cancels out exactly. This made the greedy layer-wise approach theoretically justified, not just a heuristic. The irony: pretraining turned out to be unnecessary once ReLU and good weight initialisation arrived a few years later. But it was the key that reopened the door — the paper convinced enough researchers that deep networks were worth trying again, ending the second neural network winter.

[^dnnresearch]: After AlexNet won ImageNet 2012 by a margin that stunned the field — 15.3% top-5 error versus second place's 26.2% — Hinton and his two students, Alex Krizhevsky and Ilya Sutskever, incorporated a shell company called DNNresearch and conducted an unusual auction. Hinton has a severe chronic back condition that makes sitting impossible; he refuses to fly. Rather than travel to meet bidders, the auction was conducted by laptop while he attended a conference in Lake Tahoe, moving between sessions. Google, Microsoft, DeepMind, and Baidu all bid. The price rose past $44M. Google won. Hinton had spent 25 years working against the dominant paradigm of the field, funded modestly in Toronto, watching the centre of gravity remain in symbolic AI and then SVMs. He sold the result of that persistence for more than most universities' annual research budgets.
